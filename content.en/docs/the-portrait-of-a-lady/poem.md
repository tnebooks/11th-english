## Once Upon A Time

Once upon a time, son
They used to laugh with their hearts
And laugh with their eyes:
But now they only laugh with their teeth
While their ice-block-cold eyes
Search behind my shadow.

There was a time indeed
They used to shake hands with their hearts
But that's gone, son
Now they shake hands without hearts
While their left hands search
My empty pockets.

"Feel at home!", "Come again":
They say, and when I come
Again and feel
At home, once, twice
There will be no thrice -
For then I find doors shut on me.

So I have learnt many things, son
I have learned to wear many faces
Like dresses - home face
Office face, street face, host face
Cocktail face, with all their conforming smiles
Like a fixed portrait smile.

And I have learned too
To laugh with only my teeth
And shake hands without my heart
I have also learned to say "Goodbye"
When I mean "Good-riddance":
To say "Glad to meet you"
Without being glad; and to say "It's been
Nice talking to you", after being bored.

But believe me, son
I want to be what I used to be
When I was like you. I want
To unlearn all these muting things
Most of all, I want to relearn
How to laugh, for my laugh in the mirror
Shows only my teeth like a snake's bare fangs!

So show me, son
How to laugh; show me how
I used to laugh and smile
Once upon a time when I was like you.

## About the Author

Gabriel Okara (born 1921) is a Nigerian poet and novelist. His verse has been translated into several languages. His poem *The Call of the River Nun* won the best award for Literature at the Nigerian Festival of Arts in 1953. Some of his poems were published in the influential periodical *Black Orpheus*, and by 1960 he established himself as an accomplished writer. He was honoured with the Commonwealth Poetry Award. Okara's typical poem transits from everyday reality to moments of delight and moves back to reality, making a complete circle. Okara infused African thought, folklore, and imagery into both his verse and prose. His first novel, *The Voice*, is an outstanding linguistic experiment. His later works include a collection of poems *The Fisherman's Invocation* (1978) and two books for children, *Little Snake and Little Frog* (1981) and *An Adventure to Juju Island* (1992).

## Understanding the Poem

### 1. Based on your understanding of the poem answer the following questions in one or two sentences each:

**a. What do you associate with the title of the poem?**

The title 'Once Upon a Time' associates with fairy tales and a past time that was better and more innocent, suggesting that the poet is reminiscing about a time when people were genuine and sincere.

**b. What is the relationship between the narrator and the listener?**

The narrator is a father and the listener is his son, as indicated by the repeated address "son" throughout the poem.

**c. What happens to the poet when he visits someone for the third time?**

When the poet visits someone for the third time, he finds the doors shut on him, indicating that people's hospitality is insincere and limited.

**d. Pick out the expressions that indicate conflicting ideas.**

The expressions "laugh with their hearts" and "laugh with their teeth", "shake hands with their hearts" and "shake hands without hearts" indicate conflicting ideas.

**e. How does the poet compare his face with dresses?**

The poet compares his face with dresses by saying he has learned to wear many faces like dresses - a home face, office face, street face, host face, and cocktail face.

**f. What does the poet mean when he says 'good bye'?**

When the poet says 'goodbye', he actually means 'good-riddance', which shows that he is glad to be rid of the person he is talking to.

**g. What pleasantries does the poet use to fake cordiality?**

The poet uses pleasantries like "Glad to meet you" without being glad, and "It's been nice talking to you" after being bored.

**h. What does he desire to unlearn and relearn?**

He desires to unlearn all the false and artificial ways of behaving, and most of all, he wants to relearn how to laugh genuinely like a child.

**i. How is the poet's laugh reflected in the mirror?**

The poet's laugh in the mirror shows only his teeth like a snake's bare fangs, which indicates that his laugh is hollow and frightening rather than genuine.

**j. What does the poet long for?**

The poet longs to return to his childhood innocence and genuineness, and to laugh and smile sincerely once again.

**k. Mention the qualities, the child in the poem symbolises.**

The child in the poem symbolises innocence, sincerity, genuineness, honesty, and the ability to express true emotions without pretense.

### 3. A. Interpret each of the following expressions used in the poem, in one or two lines.

**i. laugh with their eyes**

This means laughing genuinely with true emotion and happiness, where the eyes reflect the joy and sincerity of the laughter.

**ii. shake hands without hearts**

This means shaking hands without any genuine warmth or feeling, going through the motion of greeting someone without any real connection.

**iii. like a fixed portrait smile**

This refers to a permanent, artificial smile that is forced and unchanging, like a smile in a portrait painting, lacking any genuine emotion.

**iv. hands search my empty pockets**

This means that people are checking to see if the narrator has anything to offer them financially or materially, showing their selfish and calculating nature.

**v. to unlearn all these muting things**

This means to forget all the false, artificial behaviours that suppress one's true self and genuine emotions.

### C. Explain the following lines with reference to the context.

**i. Once upon a time, son They used to laugh with their hearts:**

*Context:* The poet is reminiscing about the past when people were genuine and sincere. He is telling his son about how people used to laugh with true emotion and feeling.

*Explanation:* The poet uses the phrase "once upon a time" to contrast the past with the present. In the past, people laughed with genuine happiness that came from their hearts and was reflected in their eyes. Their laughter was not fake or forced.

**ii. There will be no thrice.**

*Context:* The poet is talking about how people invite him with false hospitality, saying "Feel at home" and "Come again".

*Explanation:* The poet means that he will not visit for a third time because when he does, he finds the doors shut on him. The hospitality is only for the first two visits, showing that people's welcome is not genuine.

**iii. I have learned to wear my faces Like dresses ...**

*Context:* The poet is admitting that he has become like the hypocritical people he criticises, adapting his behaviour to different situations.

*Explanation:* The poet compares the faces he wears to dresses, meaning he changes his expression and behaviour according to the situation - a home face for family, an office face for work, a street face for public, a host face for entertaining, and a cocktail face for social events.

**iv. I want to be what I used to be.**

*Context:* The poet is expressing his desire to return to his childhood innocence and genuineness.

*Explanation:* The poet has become tired of the hypocrisy and artificiality of adult life. He wants to return to his original, authentic self - the person he was before he learned all the false behaviours of modern society. He wants to shed all the masks and be genuine again.

### 4. Answer the following questions in about 100 - 150 words each.

**i. Explain the things the poet has learnt when he grew into an adult.**

The poet has learned many things as he grew into an adult, all of which relate to the hypocrisy and artificiality of modern society. He has learned to wear many faces like dresses - a home face for family, an office face for work, a street face for public, a host face for entertaining, and a cocktail face for social events. He has learned to laugh with only his teeth, without any genuine feeling or emotion. He has also learned to shake hands without his heart, meaning he performs the gesture of greeting without any real warmth or connection. He has learned to say "Goodbye" when he actually means "Good-riddance", and to say "Glad to meet you" without being glad. He has also learned to say "It's been nice talking to you" even when he has been bored. However, he has learned all these things against his will and now regrets it deeply. He realises that these learned behaviours have made him hollow and insincere, and he wishes to unlearn them all and return to his childhood genuineness.

**ii. This poem is nothing but a criticism of modern life. Justify this statement.**

The poem 'Once Upon a Time' is indeed a sharp criticism of modern life and its hypocrisy. The poet contrasts the past, when people were genuine and sincere, with the present, where people have become artificial and calculating. In the past, people laughed with their hearts and eyes, but now they only laugh with their teeth while their ice-cold eyes hide their true intentions. Handshakes, which were once warm and meaningful, have become empty gestures without any feeling. People say welcoming words like "Feel at home" and "Come again" but these are just empty pleasantries - when you visit for the third time, you find the doors shut on you. People have become selfish, looking through your empty pockets to see what they can gain from you. The poet also criticises how adults have learned to wear different faces for different occasions, becoming hypocrites who say one thing while meaning another. The poem highlights how modern society has lost its sincerity and genuine human connection, forcing people to hide their true feelings behind masks of artificiality. The poet's desire to unlearn all these false behaviours and return to childhood innocence is a powerful criticism of the corruption of human relationships in modern life.

**iii. 'Face is the index of the mind.' Does this adage concur with the views of the poet?**

The adage "Face is the index of the mind" does not concur with the views expressed by the poet in 'Once Upon a Time'. The poet suggests quite the opposite - that people in modern society have learned to hide their true feelings behind false expressions. They have learned to wear many faces like dresses, changing their expressions according to the situation. The poet describes how people laugh with their teeth while their ice-block-cold eyes reveal nothing of the heart. They smile with conforming smiles that are fixed like a portrait, not reflecting their true emotions. The poet himself admits that he has learned to laugh with only his teeth and shake hands without his heart. He says "Glad to meet you" without being glad, and "It's been nice talking to you" after being bored. His laugh in the mirror shows only his teeth like a snake's fangs, revealing no genuine happiness. Thus, the poet clearly believes that the face is no longer an index of the mind in modern society. People have become experts at disguising their true feelings behind false expressions, making the face a mask rather than a reflection of the mind.

### 6. Parallel Reading

In the poem Once Upon a Time, a parent wishes to shed falsehood and regain true ways as a child. Here is a poem where a woman longs to become a child once again to enjoy the comfort, warmth and love she received as a child from her mother, once upon a time.

## Rock Me to Sleep

Backward, turn backward, O Time, in your flight,
Make me a child again just for tonight!
Mother, come back from the echelons shore,
Take me again to your heart as of yore;
Kiss from my forehead the furrows of care,
Smooth the few silver threads out of my hair;
Over my slumbers your loving watch keep;
Rock me to sleep, mother, - rock me to sleep!

Backward, flow backward, O tide of the years!
I am so weary of toil and of tears,
Toil without recompense, tears all in vain,
Take them, and give me my childhood again!
I have grown weary of dust and decay,
Weary of flinging my soul-wealth away;
Weary of sowing for others to reap;
Rock me to sleep, mother - rock me to sleep!

Tired of the hollow, the base, the untrue,
Mother, O mother, my heart calls for you!
Many a summer the grass has grown green,
Blossomed and faded, our faces between:
Yet, with strong yearning and passionate pain,
Long I tonight for your presence again.
Come from the silence so long and so deep;
Rock me to sleep, mother, - rock me to sleep!

Over my heart, in the days that are flown,
No love like mother-love ever has shone;
No other worship abides and endures,
Faithful, unselfish, and patient like yours:
None like a mother can charm away pain
From the sick soul and the world-weary brain.
Slumber's soft calms o'er my heavy lids creep;
Rock me to sleep, mother, - rock me to sleep!

Come, let your brown hair, just lighted with gold,
Fall on your shoulders again as of old;
Let it drop over my forehead tonight,
Shading my faint eyes away from the light;
For with its sunny-edged shadows once more
Haply will throng the sweet visions of yore;
Lovingly, softly, its bright billows sweep;
Rock me to sleep, mother, - rock me to sleep!

Mother, dear mother, the years have been long
Since I last listened your lullaby song:
Sing, then, and unto my soul it shall seem
Womanhood's years have been only a dream.
Clasped to your heart in a loving embrace,
With your light lashes just sweeping my face,
Never hereafter to wake or to weep;
Rock me to sleep, mother, - rock me to sleep!

— Elizabeth Akers Allen
